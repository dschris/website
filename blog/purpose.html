A Word On Purpose

Subheading

Posted by Chris on March XX, 2016

What stage of the script of life are you in? For myself, and many of my peers, we're in the act where you're due to graduate college and be off to a full time job; likely that job will be one that you are going into based on a Co-Op or internship you were involved in. Or maybe you're continuing your education. This is typically less likely, and is often stereotyped into the realm of medical, law, or other degrees that are famous for their post-undergrad educational endeavours. And for many, the perception seems to be that graduate school is A) not easy and B) expensive. And hey, they're not wrong. But I think we may be missing a valuable piece of the equation.

What if this isn't what you want? In my personal observation, the less "likely" your path is, the less support that exists for it; the most generic of these paths being Primary Education -> High School -> College -> Employment -> Retirement. Barring some obvious details, this is the staple lifestyle that society frames and advertises to us through an onslaught of Colonial Penn commercials. I'm looking at you, Alex. But like I said, what if this isn't what you want? You could go to a trade school and become a certified electrician, plumber, mechanic, or so forth. All respectable skills as necessary now as a blacksmith was or bartender still is. But as you're ushered through high school, chances are your guidance counselor urged you to start applying to colleges before you had ever been asked what you really wanted to do. And even if you are a particularly aware 16 year old and thought about it, it's not likely you're gonna call it right on your first try. And how could you? You really don't get educated on your whole list of options.

My personal belief is that this undue pressure, especially for a high school student, is unfair. It is however a well and noble thought that everyone that completes high school will still be seeking some greater knowledge, in whatever that form may be. But it's obviously not the case, and the usual reaction we have to that is someone didn't want to work hard enough. Someone "couldn't hack it." Certainly we don't feel this way about a PhD. Society doesn't force that expectation upon you, and therefore, it's more of a personal choice. Clearly I'm not suggesting a PhD has the same attainability as an undergrad degree, but if you see my point bear with me.

Imagine if the same pressure to get a PhD was put upon your need for an undergrad degree? Do you think you still would have gone to college for one? I'm not saying it's likely the answer is no, because chances are if you are one of my peers you've suffered through so much in college in ways you never imagined were possible. And it has certainly helped to build your personal constitution and fortitude; you've likely become the kind of person that should be here college.
